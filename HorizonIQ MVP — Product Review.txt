HorizonIQ MVP — Product Review
Scope: MVP V1.1 (Change-First) as implemented
Method: Review against product memory, retention strategy, and current user flow
Verdict: Strong strategic direction; execution validates format more than habit today.

Executive Summary
HorizonIQ has a clear wedge: personalized change intelligence, not another signal feed. The “What Changed Since Your Last Visit” feature is the right retention bet and is directionally well-built.

The MVP currently wins on concept clarity and loses on trust, time-to-value friction, and sustained retention once mock data stops moving. The product feels like a promising briefing prototype, not yet a weekly habit.

1. Clarity — 7/10
What works
Observe → Understand → Act is consistent from onboarding through signal detail.
Role-aware interests (especially for students) make personalization feel intentional.
New / Rising / Falling buckets make change scannable.
“So what for you” on signals answers the right question.
What hurts
Too many comprehension layers before value: landing hero → Observe/Understand/Act → Why HorizonIQ → 3 onboarding steps → full dashboard.
Dashboard cognitive load: hero, role lens, changed signals, skills rising, opportunities, actions — multiple parallel stories.
First vs return visit framing may confuse: first visit says “This Week,” return visit says “Since Your Last Visit,” but both can look similar if buckets overlap.
Copy overreach: landing/subheadline references jobs, funding, research ingestion that MVP does not yet perform.
Clarity risk
Users may understand what HorizonIQ is but not what to do first on the dashboard.

2. Time to Value — 6/10
What works
No login removes friction.
Role-first onboarding quickly frames relevance.
Primary action in the change hero points toward decision.
What hurts
60-second claim is aspirational, not measured: 3 onboarding steps + multi-select interests (up to 11 for students) often exceeds 60 seconds.
Value is back-loaded: users must complete onboarding before seeing any intelligence.
Interest–data mismatch risk: if selected interests lack strong mock coverage, sections feel empty — value drops sharply.
First visit shows weekly change, not visit-specific delta — the core retention hook activates only on return.
Time-to-value verdict
Users get a briefing quickly; they don’t always get their most relevant briefing quickly.

3. Trust — 5/10
What works
Mock sources are labeled.
Momentum/confidence drivers are expandable.
Change deltas and badges add transparency.
Design is calm and credible (not “AI hype dashboard”).
What hurts
Intelligence feels curated, not observed: scores and momentum can read as authoritative without live provenance.
Marketing copy outruns product reality (“analyzes jobs, funding, research…”).
Personalization depth varies: role/region copy helps, but repeat visits may feel templated if mock data is static.
No credibility anchor: no briefing date freshness indicator beyond label, no “what updated this week” operational signal.
Trust verdict
Users may enjoy the first briefing but hesitate to act or return if they suspect the numbers are decorative.

4. Retention — 6/10
What works
Correct north star: users return for change, not signals.
localStorage snapshot comparison enables return-visit diff without accounts.
Baseline snapshot trick makes second visit show rising/falling with mock data.
Grouped buckets (New / Rising / Falling) support weekly habit framing.
What hurts
Retention is device-bound: clearing storage, switching browsers, or mobile/desktop split breaks the loop.
Static mock data ceiling: after visit 2–3 with unchanged JSON, “What Changed” loses credibility.
No pull mechanism: no email digest, notification, or “your briefing updated” hook.
No action follow-up: product doesn’t close the loop (“Last week we said X — here’s what shifted”).
Weekly refresh is operational, not productized: without a visible refresh cadence, retention depends on manual ops discipline.
Retention verdict
Architecture is right; habit infrastructure is incomplete.

5. Delight — 6/10
What works
Premium, readable UI aligned with Apple/Linear/Bloomberg inspiration.
Personalized one-liners can feel like a “future analyst.”
Signal detail depth rewards curiosity.
Change-first hero is a differentiated moment vs generic trend sites.
What hurts
Few “only for me” peaks: delight depends heavily on copy, not surprising insight.
Repeat visits may feel samey without fresh data or narrative progression.
Limited emotional payoff: users see intelligence but not progress (“you’re ahead of X”).
Onboarding is functional, not memorable — competent, not remarkable.
Delight verdict
Respectful and polished; not yet magical.

Top 5 Improvements (Ranked by Impact)
1. Productize weekly change freshness (Data + UI)
Impact: Very high · Retention · Trust

Make “what changed” real and visible every week:

Manual weekly JSON refresh as a scheduled product ritual
Prominent “Updated [date]” and “X signals changed since last week” in the hero
Briefing period as a first-class UI element
Why #1: Without fresh change, the core retention promise decays after 1–2 visits. This is the difference between a demo and a habit product.

2. Radical first-dashboard focus — one decision, not six sections
Impact: Very high · Clarity · Time to value

Restructure first visit around:

What Changed (top 3 only)
Why it matters for you (one paragraph)
One primary action (everything else collapsed or below fold)
Defer skills, opportunities, and secondary signals until visit 2+ or explicit expand.

Why #2: Current dashboard teaches the product instead of delivering a decision. This directly unlocks the 60-second promise.

3. Trust layer — honest intelligence labeling
Impact: High · Trust · Retention

Replace ingestion claims with honest MVP framing: “Curated intelligence (mock)” or “Sample briefing”
Standardize source + reasoning on every signal in the hero (not only detail page)
Add plain-language “Why we believe this” under momentum/confidence
Why #3: Trust is the gate to action and return. Users won’t build a weekly habit on intelligence they don’t believe.

4. Cross-session retention hook (lightweight, no full accounts)
Impact: High · Retention

Options in order of MVP feasibility:

Email capture: “Send my weekly briefing”
Bookmarkable briefing URL with encoded preferences
“Briefing updated” browser prompt on return after weekly refresh
Why #4: localStorage alone cannot sustain weekly habit at scale. Retention needs a pull mechanism outside the session.

5. Compress onboarding to true ≤60 seconds
Impact: Medium-high · Time to value · Clarity

Smart defaults by role (pre-select 2–3 interests; user can adjust)
Consider merging region + interests when region has low differentiation impact
Show preview intelligence after step 1 (role) to reward early progress
Why #5: Friction before the dashboard is the biggest leak in the funnel. Faster path to first briefing = more users reach the retention feature at all.

Bottom Line
HorizonIQ’s strategy is correct: change-first, personalized, actionable. The MVP proves the briefing format but does not yet prove the weekly habit.

If you fix only one thing: make change visibly real every week.
If you fix two: add dashboard focus so users decide in 60 seconds, not 6 minutes.

The product is one retention loop away from a compelling MVP story — and that loop is fresh, trusted, weekly change delivered with one clear action.
